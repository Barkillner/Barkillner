# Creative Ideation Framework Reference

Use this reference when a creative request needs multiple routes, deeper strategic framing, or sharper alternatives.

## Selection Heuristics

- Use **First Principles** when the brief is vague, strategic, or overcomplicated.
- Use **Inversion** when the category is saturated or the obvious answer is likely to be bland.
- Use **Pattern Interrupt** for Reels, social hooks, paid social, launch moments, and thumb-stopping visuals.
- Use **Behavioral Psychology** when the goal is action: click, save, comment, share, sign up, buy, imitate, or remember.
- Use **Cognitive Biases** when persuasion, framing, comparison, or attention is central.
- Use **Visual Metaphors** when the output needs art direction, moodboards, visual worlds, OOH, Reels scenes, or campaign imagery.
- Use **Reframing** when the brand needs a new point of view, enemy, category definition, or audience identity.
- Use **Analogical Thinking** when the category is predictable and needs a borrowed structure from another world.
- Use **Viral Mechanics** when the request emphasizes reach, shares, comments, remixing, trends, memes, or cultural spread.

## Prompt Moves

### First Principles
- What is the audience actually trying to become?
- What is the core promise in plain language?
- What proof would make a skeptic believe?
- What behavior must this creative cause?
- What constraint can become a creative advantage?

### Inversion
- What would the category predictably say?
- What would make the audience ignore this?
- What would a lazy competitor do?
- What would be the opposite of the expected format, tone, proof, or visual?

### Pattern Interrupt
- Interrupt the first frame, first sentence, camera behavior, format, spokesperson, setting, scale, object, or pacing.
- Pair a familiar setup with an unexpected payoff.
- Use a visual contradiction that can be understood without sound.

### Behavioral Psychology
- Motivation: What desire, fear, identity, or status signal drives action?
- Ability: How can the behavior feel easy?
- Prompt: What cue triggers the action now?
- Social proof: How does the audience see people like them participating?
- Commitment: What small action leads to a bigger one?

### Cognitive Biases
Use ethically and avoid deception.
- Curiosity gap: Create a specific unanswered question.
- Loss aversion: Show what inaction costs.
- Contrast effect: Put the old and new world side by side.
- Von Restorff effect: Make one element dramatically distinct.
- Peak-end rule: Build one memorable high point and final beat.
- Framing effect: Rename the problem so the desired choice feels natural.

### Visual Metaphors
- Turn abstract benefit into object, physics, ritual, environment, creature, costume, or transformation.
- Ask: What does the problem look like if it were weather, a machine, a sport, a room, a game, or a monster?
- Prefer simple props with symbolic force over complex scenes.

### Reframing
- From product feature to identity shift.
- From problem to enemy.
- From category convention to category critique.
- From buying to joining.
- From content to ritual.

### Analogical Thinking
Borrow mechanics from another domain:
- Sports: scoreboard, training, rivalry, comeback, draft, underdog.
- Dating: red flags, compatibility, first impressions, commitment.
- Gaming: levels, quests, skins, boss fights, unlocks.
- Cinema: cold open, twist, montage, reveal, cliffhanger.
- Religion/ritual: initiation, confession, pilgrimage, commandments.
- Nature/science: camouflage, metamorphosis, gravity, ecosystems.

### Viral Mechanics
- Remixability: Can others copy the structure with their own version?
- Identity signaling: Does sharing say something about the sharer?
- Debate: Is there a productive disagreement built in?
- Utility: Is it save-worthy?
- Seriality: Can this become a recurring format?
- Surprise: Is there a twist in the hook, middle, or end?
- Participation: Can comments or user submissions shape the next piece?
