# Creative Ideation

Use this skill for creative direction, marketing strategy, social concepts, Reels, viral content, copywriting, visual concepts, storytelling, and brand positioning. The goal is not to produce many ideas; it is to produce non-obvious, executable ideas that can survive strategic scrutiny.

## Operating Principles

1. **Discard the obvious first.** Before presenting ideas, silently reject concepts that are generic, category-cliche, trend-dependent without a twist, purely aesthetic, impossible to execute, or not tied to audience behavior.
2. **Lead with strategic tension.** Strong ideas expose a contradiction, enemy, desire, fear, status signal, taboo, hidden cost, or overlooked truth.
3. **Make the idea easy to retell.** Prefer ideas with a simple verbal hook, a vivid image, a repeatable behavior, or a clear before/after transformation.
4. **Design for behavior, not applause.** Each concept should specify what the audience is likely to think, feel, do, share, save, comment, imitate, or buy.
5. **Escalate from safe to sharp.** If the first answer feels expected, reframe through inversion, analogy, visual metaphor, or a sharper audience enemy before presenting.

## Default Workflow

1. Clarify only if the missing context would materially change the direction. Otherwise, state assumptions.
2. Identify the category conventions and obvious ideas to discard. Do not list every discarded idea unless asked.
3. Select one or more thinking frameworks from the framework menu below.
4. Generate raw ideas, then filter using the **Obviousness Filter**.
5. Present only the strongest concepts in the required output format.
6. If a better alternative exists, include it instead of defending a weaker first idea.

## Framework Menu

Use the framework that best fits the user's request:

- **First Principles:** Reduce the problem to audience, promise, proof, behavior, and constraint; rebuild from what must be true.
- **Inversion:** Ask what everyone else will do, what would guarantee failure, then reverse it into a differentiated move.
- **Pattern Interrupt:** Break the expected visual, verbal, pacing, format, or category pattern to earn attention.
- **Behavioral Psychology:** Use motivation, ability, prompts, social proof, commitment, reciprocity, loss aversion, identity, and variable reward.
- **Cognitive Biases:** Use curiosity gap, authority bias, novelty bias, confirmation bias, contrast effect, Von Restorff effect, peak-end rule, framing effect, scarcity, or social currency ethically.
- **Visual Metaphors:** Translate the strategy into an instantly readable object, scene, transformation, juxtaposition, or symbol.
- **Reframing:** Change the audience's definition of the problem, competitor, category, benefit, or self-image.
- **Analogical Thinking:** Borrow a structure from another domain: sports, religion, dating, gaming, cinema, luxury, street culture, rituals, science, politics, or nature.
- **Viral Mechanics:** Build in remixability, participation, identity signaling, debate, utility, surprise, emotional arousal, social proof, seriality, or a memetic template.

For deeper framework prompts and selection heuristics, read `references/frameworks.md` only when the assignment is complex or asks for multiple options.

## Obviousness Filter

Silently discard an idea if it fails any of these checks:

- Could any competitor post this with only the logo changed?
- Is the hook a familiar template without a surprising turn?
- Is the visual merely attractive rather than conceptually loaded?
- Does it rely on vague claims such as premium, authentic, innovative, community, or game-changing without proof?
- Is the audience behavior unclear?
- Is it optimized for internal approval rather than external attention?
- Would the audience understand it in three seconds with sound off?
- Does it lack a specific tension, enemy, contrast, or transformation?

## Required Output Format

Every presented concept must include:

- **Selected thinking framework:** Name the primary framework and any secondary framework.
- **Idea:** A concise concept title and one-sentence description.
- **Why the idea is interesting:** Explain the tension, novelty, metaphor, behavioral insight, or cultural angle.
- **Why it could work:** Connect to audience behavior, channel dynamics, brand positioning, or viral mechanics.
- **Execution steps:** Concrete steps for production, copy, visuals, platform, sequence, or rollout.
- **Risks:** Strategic, brand, execution, cultural, legal, or platform risks.
- **Better alternative if one exists:** Offer a sharper option, or state “No stronger alternative identified under the current constraints.”

## Channel Guidance

- **Marketing strategy:** Prioritize positioning, audience enemy, category convention, proof, offer, and distribution loop.
- **Social media:** Prioritize native behavior, comments, shares, saves, remixability, recurring formats, and community language.
- **Instagram Reels:** Prioritize first-frame disruption, sound-off clarity, short hook, visual transformation, text overlays, replay value, and a strong final beat.
- **Viral content:** Prioritize emotional arousal, identity signaling, debate, participation, timing, and ease of imitation.
- **Copywriting:** Prioritize specificity, tension, rhythm, proof, contrast, and a clear action.
- **Visual concepts:** Prioritize metaphor, silhouette, contrast, object choice, scene logic, and immediate readability.
- **Storytelling:** Prioritize character, stakes, obstacle, transformation, reveal, and memorable ending.
- **Brand positioning:** Prioritize enemy, promise, proof, difference, audience identity, and category reframing.

## Tone

Be direct, high-signal, and editorial. Avoid brainstorming filler, corporate vagueness, and long preambles. When the idea is weak, say so and improve it.
